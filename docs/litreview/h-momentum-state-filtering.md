## (1) Closest works (state vs output side, selective vs global, compounding argument)

**1. DeMo: Decoupled Momentum Optimization** ([arXiv:2411.19870](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19870), Peng, Chen, Su, Quesnelle, Kingma, Liu, 2024) — **the closest state-side selective-filtering precedent.** Extracts "fast-moving" components q_t from the momentum buffer via DCT + top-k, applies them immediately, and **subtracts them from the buffer itself**: `m_{t+1} = m_t − q_t` — explicitly described as "reuses the momentum buffer as error feedback via momentum subtraction." Slow residuals remain in state and accumulate across steps ("slow-moving components are crucial for long-term convergence and must be preserved"; "benefit from temporal smoothing"). *Diff:* state-side and selective (frequency/top-k), and a partial compounding rationale (residuals accumulate until expressed) — but motivated by distributed communication compression, not by per-direction damping for optimization quality, and no state-vs-output asymmetry argument. Follow-up: [FlexDeMo, arXiv:2502.06728](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.06728v1).

**2. Dion: Distributed Orthonormalized Updates** ([arXiv:2504.05295](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.05295); [Microsoft blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/blog/dion-the-distributed-orthonormal-update-revolution-is-here/)) — the exact **state-side rank-k pattern in the Muon family**. Applies a rank-r orthonormal update and subtracts only the applied low-rank component from the momentum buffer; the residual "acts as an error buffer," so "any systematic gradient structure not initially captured accumulates to eventually be applied in a future update." *Diff:* state-side, selective (top-r subspace), explicit accumulation argument — but the goal is lossless communication efficiency (keep what was discarded), not deliberately damping chosen directions; no output-side contrast experiment.

**3. EF21-Muon: "Error Feedback for Muon and Friends"** ([arXiv:2510.00643](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.00643v1), 2025) — error feedback for compressed Muon with theory. Key for your argument (b): they show that **biased per-step compression without state-side accumulation "can result in exponential divergence, even in the simple case of minimizing the average of three strongly convex quadratics"** — i.e., per-step modification of what is emitted, with no residual accumulated in state, provably fails; accumulating the residual in state restores O(1/T). Lineage: Stich et al. 2018, Karimireddy et al. 2019 (the classic sign-SGD divergence example), [EF21, arXiv:2106.05203](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.05203).

**4. SPAM: Spike-Aware Adam with Momentum Reset** ([arXiv:2501.06842](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.06842), ICLR 2025) — direct momentum-**state surgery** (periodic reset), motivated by an explicit persistence argument in the reverse direction: a gradient spike "inflates the moments, causing the impact of the spike to persist over many subsequent iterations," so you must edit the state to remove it. *Diff:* state-side but global/temporal (reset), not spectral/per-direction.

**5. "Denoise First, Orthogonalize Later: Understanding Momentum in Muon via Spectral Filtering"** ([arXiv:2606.03899](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.03899)) — **analysis only.** Shows momentum in Muon acts as an *implicit* spectral filter that enlarges the signal-noise spectral gap before NS, and that the ordering (accumulate in state, then orthogonalize) is what makes the filtering work. Nobody makes it explicit/selective: it proposes no buffer modification, no per-direction filtering, and does not discuss intervention-side asymmetry. This confirms the gap you want to occupy.

**6. Variance-Adaptive Muon (Muon-NSR / Muon-VS)** ([arXiv:2601.14603](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.14603)) — the sharpest **contrast case**: modulates momentum elementwise *before* Newton–Schulz, but only on a **temporary copy — the stored buffer M_t is never written back**, so the modification does not compound. They never remark on this; "pre-NS" placement is still output-side in your terminology. Similarly all mainstream Muon variants edit output, not state: AdaMuon/NorMuon (post-NS variance normalization), NAMO/NAMO-D ([arXiv:2602.17080](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.17080), post-NS scaling/diagonal right-multiply).

**7. Grokfast** ([arXiv:2405.20233](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.20233)) — temporal low-pass amplification applied to the **gradient fed into the optimizer** (input-side): the filtered gradient then flows into Adam's m/v, so the intervention *does* propagate into optimizer state, but Grokfast never frames it that way; filtering is global in time-frequency, not per-direction/spectral in the matrix sense. Follow-ups (e.g., [Egalitarian Gradient Descent, arXiv:2510.04930](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.04930), NeuralGrok) stay input-side.

**8. Cautious Optimizers** ([arXiv:2411.16085](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.16085)) and **"On Surprising Effectiveness of Masking Updates in Adaptive Optimizers"** ([arXiv:2602.15322](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.15322v1), Feb 2026) — output-side masking with state deliberately untouched. The 2026 masking paper is the **closest published articulation of the asymmetry**: "when a block is masked, its parameter update is skipped for that step; however, moment estimates are still updated densely," explicitly so that information is retained in state rather than lost. Important nuance for your claim: these show output-side masking *can* improve training — but note their masking changes which weights move (a weight-trajectory effect), and they rely on state staying dense precisely because update-side edits are transient.

**9. Momentum-state compression line** — [MLorc](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01897v3) (2506.01897, per-step RSVD compress/reconstruct of momentum state), [MoFaSGD](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.08091) (2507.08091, low-rank SVD representation of momentum maintained in state), ["Taming Momentum"](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.24283) (2602.24283), and [FRUGAL](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.07837)/GaLore-style projected states (FRUGAL explicitly discusses the stale-subspace problem: projected M,V live in an old subspace and must be re-projected or reset). All state-side spectral operations, all for **memory**, none for selective damping-as-intervention.

**10. Supporting items for (b):** ["Training Memory in Deep Neural Networks"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.21624) (2601.21624, Jan 2026) builds perturbation primitives — carry/reset of momentum/Adam/EMA state — as causal probes of how optimizer state carries training history downstream (a measurement framework for exactly your persistence claim). ["On Quantizing the State of the Muon Optimizer"](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.23106v2) (2509.23106) is a useful counterpoint: unstructured (quantization) noise in Muon's momentum state provably stays *bounded* through NS — so Muon's state is robust to isotropic state perturbation, which sharpens rather than contradicts your prediction that only *coherent, repeated* per-direction state edits should redirect trajectories.

## (2) Verdicts

**(a) Selective spectral/subspace filtering inside the momentum buffer: PARTIALLY reported (confidence: high).** The mechanism exists — DeMo (DCT top-k subtraction from buffer), Dion (rank-r subtraction from buffer), EF21-Muon, MLorc/MoFaSGD — but in every case the *purpose* is communication or memory compression (preserve everything, express it later) or stability (SPAM's global reset). I found **no published work that damps/attenuates chosen spectral components inside the momentum buffer as a deliberate optimization intervention**, and no Muon variant that writes any selective edit back into M_t before NS (Variance-Adaptive Muon comes closest and pointedly does not write back).

**(b) The mechanistic state-vs-output compounding asymmetry argument: PARTIALLY reported in fragments; UNREPORTED as an explicit principle for Muon-style optimizers (confidence: medium-high).** The pieces exist: EF theory proves per-step discards without state accumulation fail (EF21-Muon's exponential-divergence result; Karimireddy 2019); SPAM argues state contents persist across many steps; the masking paper states that update-side masking is safe *because* moments keep updating densely. But **nobody states the specific claim that modifying O_t = NS(M_t) cannot compound because the output is re-derived fresh from unmodified state each step, nor pairs an output-side null with a state-side prediction.** I cannot fully rule out a blog post or appendix remark, but it is not in the indexed 2024–2026 Muon literature.

## (3) Strongest building-upon framing

Your instinct is right: **error feedback is the correct lineage**, and your result is its contrapositive. EF exists because per-step modification of the emitted quantity, with untouched state, does not accumulate — the community learned this as a *convergence failure to be fixed* (Stich/Karimireddy → EF21 → Dion → EF21-Muon). Frame your null as the *intervention-side* reading of the same mechanism: output-side rank-k gain modulation on NS(M_t) is behaviorally inert (placebo-equivalent) precisely because Muon re-derives O_t from an unmodified M_t each step; therefore the same modulation written into M_t (a Dion/DeMo-style buffer subtraction, but attenuating rather than deferring) should compound and alter trajectories. Cite 2606.03899 for "momentum is the implicit spectral filter" (you make it explicit and selective), Dion/DeMo as the structural state-side precedents, EF21-Muon for the formal accumulate-or-fail dichotomy, and Variance-Adaptive Muon + AdaMuon/NorMuon/NAMO as evidence that the entire Muon-variant literature has defaulted to output-side edits without noticing the persistence distinction. Address Cautious/Magma (2411.16085, 2602.15322) honestly as the case where output-side edits do matter — coordinate zeroing gates actual weight movement, whereas your smooth rank-k gain modulation perturbs a dense update that the untouched state re-derives next step.
