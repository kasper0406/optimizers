## Literature scan: "schedules as feedback control in disguise" / deriving schedules from an online-statistic setpoint

### (1) Closest works (ranked by proximity to your claim)

**1. Convergence-diagnostic step sizes — Pflug (1983/1990); Chee & Toulis, AISTATS 2018 ([1710.06382](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1710.06382)); Pesme et al., ICML 2020 ([2007.00534](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00534))**
The single closest lineage. Pflug's diagnostic: the running average of the inner product of *successive stochastic gradients* is positive in the transient phase and turns negative at stationarity (oscillation around the optimum) — i.e., the sign of a lag-1 gradient autocorrelation is used as the control signal to trigger LR drops. Chee & Toulis and Pesme et al. build SGD step-size schemes on it; Pesme et al. also prove the raw Pflug statistic doesn't concentrate well even on quadratics.
**Diff vs yours:** scalar, whole-gradient, convex/SGD setting; the statistic is binary (crossed zero → halve LR), used as a *trigger*, not a continuous setpoint; no per-direction decomposition, no occupancy fraction, no claim that classical schedules (cosine/WSD) implicitly regulate this statistic, nothing on Muon/deep nets. Nobody in this line inverts it into "cosine ≡ constant-occupancy trajectory."

**2. SASA / SASA+ — Lang, Xiao, Zhang, NeurIPS 2019 ([paper](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper_files/paper/2019/file/e1054bf2d703bca1e8fe101d3ac5efcd-Paper.pdf))**
Formal statistical stationarity test on an online statistic of SGD-with-momentum dynamics; drop LR by a constant factor when stationary. Explicitly "statistics automate the schedule."
**Diff:** again a stationarity *trigger* producing step schedules; statistic is a scalar equilibrium relation, not temporal autocorrelation occupancy; no reverse-engineering of why smooth schedules work.

**3. Yaida's fluctuation–dissipation scheduler, ICLR 2019 ([1810.00004](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.00004), [code](https://github.com/facebookresearch/FDR_scheduler))**
Derives exact stationary FDR relations linking measurable quantities to hyperparameters; adaptively decays LR when the measured FDR ratio indicates equilibration. This is the cleanest prior instance of "derive the schedule from the requirement that a measured online statistic sit at its stationary value."
**Diff:** the statistic is an equilibrium FDR ratio (norms/inner products), not per-direction lag-1 autocorrelation; produces step-decay control, and does not claim standard cosine/WSD schedules are implicitly holding the statistic on a trajectory; pre-dates Muon and per-direction spectral decompositions.

**4. Edge of stability / central flows — Cohen et al. 2021 ([2103.00065](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2103.00065)); Cohen, Damian et al., ICLR 2025 "Central Flows" ([2410.24206](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.24206), [site](https://centralflows.github.io/))**
The strongest "state function" precedent: sharpness equilibrates to ≈ 2/η via a *negative feedback loop implemented by oscillations* — so under a schedule, λ_max(t) tracks 2/η(t) largely independent of history. Central flows model the time-averaged trajectory plus the oscillation covariance explicitly. This is exactly "a measured dynamics statistic is a state function of instantaneous LR" — but for curvature, and it's an emergent property of GD, not a principle from which schedules are derived.
**Diff:** they show LR *causes* a statistic to sit at a setpoint; they do not invert this into "good schedule shapes are the ones holding X constant," and the statistic is curvature, not the *fraction of oscillating directions* (occupancy). Your occupancy is plausibly the EoS oscillation phenomenon seen through a different, cheaper observable — a framing you must explicitly differentiate from (does occupancy carry information beyond λ_max·η/2?).

**5. CDAT — Roulet et al., NeurIPS 2024 ([2407.06183](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.06183v1))**
Explicit "closed-loop feedback between LR tuning and curvature" framing: greedy LR tuners fail because they *break* the sharpness-stabilizing feedback; CDAT targets curvature stabilization and spontaneously reproduces warmup-like schedules. The nearest published statement of "schedule shapes emerge from holding a dynamics statistic stable."
**Diff:** statistic = sharpness/stability ratio, full-batch-centric; reproduces warmup, not cosine/WSD anneals; no autocorrelation, no per-direction occupancy, no stochastic-oscillation classifier.

**6. River-valley WSD — Wen, Kokolis et al. 2024 ([2410.05192](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.05192)); also "Through the River" ([2507.09846](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.09846)) and WSD cooldown dynamics ([2508.01483](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.01483))**
Reverse-engineers WSD via geometry: stable phase = large oscillations in sharp "mountain" directions while progressing along the river; decay = oscillation suppression revealing hidden progress. This is qualitatively your occupancy-collapse-during-anneal observation, told as a landscape story.
**Diff:** qualitative/theoretical; no online oscillation statistic is measured or held at a setpoint; schedule is explained, not derived from a measured invariant. Your occupancy metric would be a direct *quantitative instrument* for their picture.

**7. Multi-power law / functional-scaling-law schedule derivation — Luo et al. 2025 ([2503.12811](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.12811)); optimal schedules under FSL ([2602.06797](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.06797)); random-feature optimal-schedule theory ([2602.04774](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.04774)); Tissue et al. "Scaling Law with LR Annealing" ([2408.11029](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.11029v1))**
The 2024–2026 "derive the schedule" line: fit loss-as-functional-of-lr-trajectory (forward area S1 + annealing area S2, or multi-power kernels), then optimize the schedule shape; WSD/power-decay emerge as optima. Tissue et al.'s L(s) = L0 + A·S1^(−α) − C·S2 is literally "loss is a state function of the lr trajectory."
**Diff:** the fitted object is the *loss curve*, a black-box functional — no mechanistic online statistic, no autocorrelation, no feedback-control interpretation. Complementary rather than overlapping: they predict, you'd explain the predictor.

**8. LR-as-temperature / noise-scale line — Mandt-Hoffman-Blei 2017; Smith & Le; "Don't decay the LR, increase the batch size" (2018); trajectory invariance ([2509.25049](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.25049))**
Classic view: annealing lr ≡ annealing SGD temperature g ∝ ηN/B; schedules are temperature trajectories; 2025's trajectory-invariance shows loss/grad-noise/grad-norm curves are invariant along a combined lr–wd direction.
**Diff:** temperature is an *input-side* quantity (set by hyperparameters), not a measured emergent statistic; constant-temperature would say occupancy should track ηN/B — your constant-occupancy is only equivalent if occupancy is a monotone function of temperature alone, which per your EoS-adjacent data (deterministic oscillation component) it likely is not. This is the discriminating experiment reviewers will ask for.

**9. Feedback-controller schedulers — PID LR scheduler (IEEE TNNLS 2022, [pubmed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36288221/)); GALA gradient-alignment LR adaptation ([2506.08419](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08419)); "Revisiting Learning Rate Control" 2025 ([2507.01724](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.01724)); SALR ([2011.05348](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2011.05348))**
Control-the-LR-from-a-signal methods: PID on loss; GALA raises/lowers LR from *consecutive-gradient alignment* (a lag-1 correlation signal!) plus local curvature; SALR from sharpness. The 2025 survey (2507.01724) benchmarks "online scheduling based on gradient statistics" as a category and finds none reliably beats tuned fixed schedules.
**Diff:** all are forward controllers proposing *new* adaptive schemes; none makes the inverse claim — that classical cosine/WSD schedules already *are* the closed-loop solution holding a specific measured statistic constant — and none uses a per-direction oscillation-occupancy fraction. GALA is the closest signal-wise (consecutive-gradient alignment) but scalar/greedy, no setpoint principle, no schedule reverse-engineering.

**10. Muon-specific dynamics — "Spectral Dynamics and Noise Geometry of Muon" ([2606.08388](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.08388)); "Spectral Flattening Is All Muon Needs" ([2605.13079](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.13079))**
Measure Muon's singular-value dynamics and noise geometry; nothing on schedules, oscillation autocorrelation, or statistic-derived LR. Confirms the Muon × schedule × oscillation-statistic intersection is empty in the literature as of mid-2026.

### (2) Novelty verdict: **PARTIALLY REPORTED — the specific claim is unreported.** Confidence: moderately high (extensive 2024–2026 sweep; caveat: cannot rule out a very recent workshop paper).

- **Reported:** (a) an online lag-1-type gradient statistic as an LR *control trigger* (Pflug/Chee–Toulis/SASA/Yaida — the "derive LR changes from a stationarity statistic" concept per se is 30+ years old); (b) a dynamics statistic being a state function of instantaneous LR (sharpness ≈ 2/η, central flows); (c) schedules-as-feedback-control framing in one direction (CDAT: warmup emerges from curvature stabilization); (d) schedule shapes derived by optimizing a fitted loss functional (multi-power/FSL); (e) qualitative "oscillation collapse during anneal" (river valley).
- **Open / unreported:** the *specific composite*: (i) a **per-direction oscillation-occupancy fraction** (fraction of projections with lag-1 autocorr < threshold) as the tracked statistic; (ii) demonstrating occupancy(t) is a **state function of lr(t) under standard schedules** (path-independence test); (iii) **inverting** that into a constant-occupancy (or occupancy-trajectory) principle from which cosine/WSD are *derived*; (iv) any of this under **Muon/orthogonalized updates**. No paper found claims "good schedules implicitly hold a measured temporal-autocorrelation statistic at a setpoint."

### (3) Strongest building-upon angle
Position occupancy as the **stochastic, cheap, per-direction generalization of the edge-of-stability order parameter**, and your result as the quantitative closed-loop reading of the river-valley picture: central flows prove oscillations regulate sharpness to 2/η in the deterministic limit; river valley says WSD works by managing mountain-direction oscillation; you supply the measurable control variable (occupancy) and the inverse derivation (schedule from setpoint). Two must-run discriminators the literature hands you: (a) show occupancy is not just a monotone repackaging of λ_max·η/2 (else Cohen et al. subsumes it) and not just of temperature ηN/B (else Smith–Le subsumes it) — e.g., vary batch size at fixed lr; (b) benchmark the derived constant-occupancy schedule against multi-power-law-optimal schedules (2503.12811) — matching their shape from a mechanistic principle rather than a curve fit would be the headline.
