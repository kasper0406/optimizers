# Literature report: gradient temporal-autocorrelation as a feedback signal for global LR control

## (1) Closest works, ranked by proximity to the proposal

**1. Chee & Toulis, "Convergence diagnostics for stochastic gradient descent with constant learning rate," AISTATS 2018 (arXiv:1710.06382); building on Pflug (1983).**
Maintains a running sum of the inner product of *successive stochastic gradients* ⟨g_t, g_{t+1}⟩. In the transient phase this is positive in expectation (gradients keep pointing the same way); in the stationary phase SGD oscillates around the optimum and the inner product turns *negative in expectation*. When the statistic crosses zero, the LR is halved and the test restarts. **Match:** this is exactly "lag-1 anticorrelation of gradients ⇒ LR is now too large relative to signal ⇒ act on global LR." It is the single closest ancestor of your idea. **Differences:** (a) the statistic is a single full-parameter-vector inner product, not a per-direction occupancy *fraction*; (b) it is a one-sided stationarity *detector* driving a monotone decay schedule (halve-on-alarm), not a bidirectional setpoint controller holding a target value; (c) plain SGD, convex-ish settings — nothing about normalized/orthogonalized optimizers; (d) notably, Pesme, Dieuleveut & Flammarion, "On Convergence-Diagnostic based Step Sizes for SGD," ICML 2020 (arXiv:2007.00534) showed the Pflug statistic has too much variance to be a reliable test even on quadratics and replaced it with a distance-based diagnostic — a documented failure mode your occupancy-fraction formulation (a binned, per-direction, thresholded statistic, i.e., a variance-reduced order parameter) directly addresses.

**2. Jiang, Kavis & Mokhtari, "Online Learning-guided Learning Rate Adaptation via Gradient Alignment" (GALA), arXiv:2506.08419, 2025.**
Adapts a *single global scalar LR* by feeding the alignment (inner product) of consecutive gradients, normalized with a local curvature estimate, into an online learner (FTRL): LR rises when consecutive gradients align, falls when they anti-align. Proves a data-adaptive convergence rate for *normalized SGD* + GALA; experiments on SGD/Adam show robustness to initial LR. **Match:** global LR controller with consecutive-gradient temporal correlation as the feedback signal — the closest *modern controller* to your proposal, including a normalized-optimizer flavor in theory. **Differences:** signal is a single aggregate alignment scalar, not the *occupancy fraction of directions* below an autocorrelation threshold; no setpoint/target semantics (regret-minimization framing, not "hold statistic at ρ*"); no Muon/orthogonalized matrix optimizers; no claim connecting the statistic to criticality/edge-of-stability, and no observation like your LR-monotone, momentum-independent occupancy.

**3. Schoenauer-Sebag, Schoenauer & Sebag, "Stochastic Gradient Descent: Going As Fast As Possible But Not Faster" (SALeRA), arXiv:1709.01427, 2017.**
Global LR controller with an "as fast as possible but not faster" philosophy: compares the momentum of *normalized* gradient vectors against the null model of random unit vectors (an agreement/anti-correlation statistic) to gently increase or decrease a global LR, plus a change-detection test that halves LR on catastrophic episodes. **Match:** explicitly treats gradient anti-correlation vs. a stochastic null as the global LR feedback signal; philosophically the same "critical tuning" goal. **Differences:** aggregate statistic over the whole gradient vector, not per-direction occupancy; hypothesis-test-and-nudge, not setpoint tracking; pre-Muon, plain SGD; largely uncited in the modern LLM-optimizer literature.

**4. Baydin, Cornish, Rubio, Schmidt & Wood, "Online Learning Rate Adaptation with Hypergradient Descent," ICLR 2018 (arXiv:1703.04782).**
The global-LR hypergradient is exactly ⟨g_t, g_{t−1}⟩, so the method does gradient ascent on the LR proportional to the lag-1 gradient correlation: sustained positive correlation ⇒ LR grows, anti-correlation ⇒ LR shrinks. Applied to SGD, SGD-momentum, Adam. **Match:** global LR, lag-1 correlation signal, online. **Differences:** no target statistic (equilibrium is implicitly ⟨g_t,g_{t−1}⟩ = 0, i.e., *zero* autocorrelation, whereas you hold a *negative*-autocorrelation occupancy at a nonzero target — a materially different fixed point); scalar aggregate rather than direction-resolved fraction; known to be brittle (needs its own hyper-LR); not for Muon-family.

**5. Jacobs' delta-bar-delta (1988) and Riedmiller & Braun's Rprop (1993) — the sign-flip lineage (also Almeida et al. 1998; RMSprop's origin as "mini-batch Rprop," Tieleman & Hinton 2012).**
Per-weight gain adaptation from the sign agreement of the current gradient with an EMA of past gradients (delta-bar-delta) or with the previous gradient (Rprop): consistent sign ⇒ additively increase that weight's step; sign flip ⇒ multiplicatively decrease. **How close, exactly:** the *signal* is the lag-1 sign statistic of the per-coordinate gradient — the coordinate-wise ancestor of your per-direction autocorrelation. **Differences are structural, not cosmetic:** (a) *granularity* — per-weight independent gains, no global LR, no aggregate occupancy statistic; (b) *no setpoint* — each coordinate is pushed toward zero flipping (they treat oscillation purely as an error to eliminate, whereas you treat a controlled level of anti-correlation as the *desired operating point*); (c) full-batch heritage — Rprop notoriously breaks under mini-batch noise because a single-sample sign flip is noise, which is why RMSprop replaced signs with magnitude normalization; your EMA'd, thresholded, population-level fraction is again the variance-robust version; (d) nothing on projected/spectral directions or orthogonalized optimizers. **Verdict on this lineage: it establishes sign-flip feedback per weight as 40-year-old prior art, but no work in it aggregates flip statistics into a global occupancy signal or targets a nonzero level.**

**6. Lang, Xiao & Zhang, "Using Statistics to Automate Stochastic Optimization," NeurIPS 2019 (arXiv:1909.09785) and Zhang et al., SALSA, arXiv:2002.10597, 2020; underpinned by Yaida, "Fluctuation-dissipation relations for SGD," ICLR 2019.**
Online stationarity test built from Yaida's fluctuation-dissipation relation (an exact stationary-state identity between observables like ⟨θ·g⟩ and step-size-weighted second moments); when the test statistic says the constant-LR chain has equilibrated, LR is cut by a constant factor; SALSA adds a smoothed line-search warmup. **Match:** "hold a measured statistic of optimizer dynamics, act on global LR" — the closest in *spirit* to statistic-targeted control, and the FDR is mathematically adjacent to autocorrelation of the iterates. **Differences:** detect-equilibrium-then-decay (monotone schedule), not a setpoint held throughout; statistic is a stationarity residual, not oscillation occupancy; SGD/momentum, not normalized optimizers.

**7. Roulet, Agarwala, Grill, Swirszcz, Blondel & Pedregosa, "Stepping on the Edge: Curvature Aware Learning Rate Tuners" (CDAT), arXiv:2407.06183, 2024.**
Shows greedy LR tuners break the edge-of-stability self-stabilization and proposes CDAT, which chooses the global LR to prioritize *long-term stabilization of sharpness* (effectively holding the system at the edge, i.e., ηλ_max ≈ 2), recovering warmup-like behavior for free. Related: Damian, Vaskevicius, Lee "Self-Stabilization" (2023) — the loss's cubic term is itself a negative feedback controller holding λ ≈ 2/η; Cohen et al. "Central Flows" (arXiv:2410.24206) — the time-averaged theory of exactly the oscillations you measure; Agarwala et al. on Adam at the edge (arXiv:2207.14484). **Match:** this cluster is the strongest precedent for "automatic critical tuning" — global LR chosen by feedback to hold the dynamics at the stability boundary. **Differences:** the feedback signal is *curvature* (λ_max via Hessian access — expensive, and ill-defined per-direction for Muon's preconditioned geometry), not a cheap gradient temporal statistic; your negative-autocorrelation occupancy is precisely the observable *signature* of ηλ > 1 oscillation, so your controller is arguably CDAT's objective implemented with an O(1)-overhead measurable proxy. None of this line handles Muon/orthogonalized updates.

**8. Distance-based parameter-free methods — Defazio & Mishchenko, D-Adaptation (ICML 2023); Mishchenko & Defazio, Prodigy (arXiv:2306.06101); Ivgi, Hinder & Carmon, DoG (ICML 2023); Khaled et al., DoWG (NeurIPS 2023).**
Estimate the initial distance-to-solution D from iterate trajectories/gradient sums and set the global LR ≈ D/√(Σ‖g‖²). **Explicit distinction per your brief:** the signal is a *spatial* quantity (distance traveled / gradient norms), not a *temporal* statistic; there is no oscillation feedback and no setpoint; convex theory, and known to interact awkwardly with normalized optimizers (Muon's update norm is fixed, so gradient-norm-based denominators lose meaning — which is an argument *for* your temporal signal in that family).

**9. Muon-specific LR tuning, 2025–2026.**
What exists: muP-style transfer for Muon (Essential AI "Practical Efficiency of Muon for Pretraining," arXiv:2505.02222 — clean LR/WD transfer to 3.7B with RMS-scaling s=0.2√n; "Extending µP: Spectral Conditions for Feature Learning Across Optimizers," arXiv:2602.20937; "Weight Decay may matter more than muP for LR Transfer," arXiv:2510.19093); static analyses of why Muon tolerates large LRs ("Spectral Flattening Is All Muon Needs," arXiv:2605.13079; "The Spectral Dynamics and Noise Geometry of Muon," arXiv:2606.08388 — notes a broad LR plateau, measures spectra, but **no temporal autocorrelation measurement and no online LR control**); AdaMuon (arXiv:2507.11005) and "Adam Improves Muon" (arXiv:2602.17080) — per-parameter second-moment modulation *inside* the update, not a global controller from a temporal statistic; Muown row-norm control (arXiv:2605.10797) — widens the LR plateau structurally. **I found no Muon-family work that measures gradient/projection autocorrelation, and none that closes a feedback loop from any online statistic to the global LR.**

**Honorable mentions / adjacent but distinct:** Schaul, Zhang & LeCun "No More Pesky Learning Rates" (2013) — per-dimension LR from gradient mean²/variance (SNR, not temporal correlation); Nagel et al. "Overcoming Oscillations in Quantization-Aware Training" (ICML 2022) — per-weight oscillation-*frequency* detection with an explicit threshold f_th, but used to freeze/dampen weights in QAT, not to control LR; Orvieto et al. anticorrelated-noise-injection — injects anticorrelation, doesn't measure it; McCandlish et al. gradient-noise-scale — batch-size, not LR; RL/learned LR controllers (arXiv:1705.11159, event-based E/PD control arXiv:2003.09503) — loss-based state, not gradient statistics. Two striking cross-domain precedents for your exact principle: **Mehra (1970) adaptive Kalman filtering** — adapt the gain until the innovation sequence's autocorrelation is white (nonzero lag-1 autocorr in either sign ⇒ gain is off-optimal), and **van Beers et al.'s dart-throwing motor-control work (PLoS ONE 2013)** — human movement corrections show lag-1 endpoint autocorrelation ≈ 0 at the optimal correction gain, negative when the gain is too high. Both hold an autocorrelation statistic at a target by adjusting a gain — in other fields, and both target *zero*, not a calibrated negative-occupancy fraction.

## (2) Novelty verdict: **PARTIALLY REPORTED** (confidence: high on the parts below, moderate that nothing was missed in very recent preprints)

**Reported already:** (a) lag-1 gradient correlation/sign-flip as an LR-adaptation signal — per-weight (delta-bar-delta/Rprop, 1988/1993), global-scalar (Pflug/Chee-Toulis, hypergradient descent, SALeRA, GALA); (b) "keep the system at the stability edge via LR feedback" as a goal (CDAT, self-stabilization theory); (c) statistical tests on optimizer dynamics driving LR drops (SALSA/Yaida).

**Not found anywhere (the open part):** (i) using the **occupancy fraction of per-direction projections with autocorrelation below a threshold** — a population-level order parameter rather than a scalar inner product — as the controlled variable; (ii) a **setpoint controller holding that statistic at a nonzero target** (all prior art either targets zero correlation implicitly or uses one-way decay-on-alarm); (iii) **any** temporal-autocorrelation-based LR control for **Muon/orthogonalized/normalized matrix optimizers**, where you can argue the signal is uniquely well-suited (update norms are fixed, so norm- and distance-based signals are degenerate); (iv) your empirical characterization itself (occupancy 60–89%, monotone in LR across configs and within-run, momentum-independent) appears unreported.

## (3) Strongest building-upon angle
Frame the contribution as: **a cheap, measurable, variance-robust proxy that implements edge-of-stability targeting for optimizers where curvature feedback is impractical.** Concretely: (a) position the occupancy fraction as the fix for the *documented* variance failure of the Pflug/Chee-Toulis scalar statistic (Pesme et al. 2020 is your motivating negative result); (b) position the controller as achieving CDAT's objective (long-term stabilization at criticality) without Hessian-vector products, and in Muon's preconditioned geometry where "sharpness at 2/η" isn't even the right invariant; (c) exploit the muP-transfer literature: if occupancy-at-target is scale-invariant, your controller is a *dynamic* alternative/complement to static µP LR transfer — that comparison (controller vs. transferred-then-fixed LR across widths) is untouched territory; (d) the Kalman innovation-whiteness analogy (Mehra 1970) gives a principled control-theoretic pedigree for "hold autocorrelation at target by adjusting gain" and explains why a *nonzero negative* target (critical tuning, not whitening) is the interesting deep-learning-specific twist. Mandatory baselines to differentiate against in the paper: GALA, hypergradient descent, Prodigy/D-Adaptation, and a Chee-Toulis-style halving schedule.
